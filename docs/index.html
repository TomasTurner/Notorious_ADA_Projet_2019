Why is this important?

Harmful use of alcohol causes more than 5% of the global disease burden. The death of more than 3 million people a year is caused by the substance. Simultaneously it is source of a 1344 billion dollar industry, and an important cultural factor. This makes it a highly important, and highly complex, matter for regulators which have to take into account many aspects when creating policies. Alcohol control policies exist since many years, from taxes to total bans. The World Health Organization (WHO) has gathered significant amount of data on both alcohol consumption and related policies in different countries. This data can be used to evaluate the effectiveness of the policies and later policymakers can use it to make informed decisions.

Research Questions

  • What kind of legislation is the most common in which type of country?
  • Which policies correlate the most with low alcohol consumption?
  • Does policies or socio-economic factor explain better different levels of alcohol consumption?

Analysis of alcohol policies

From the data from the World Health Organization allows one can identify three major means frequently used by states in their politics of alcohol consumption limitations and reduction:
1. charge money for alcohol production, retail and consumption. This is an interesting feature since the effectiveness of tax policy interventions for reducing excessive alcohol consumption is subjected to a lot of studies. Results tend to show a strong positive impact, which according to this study is proportional to the magnitude of the taxation.
2. restrict access to alcohol in public places (e.g. in public transport, workplaces, places of worship, or during sporting events, etc)
3. raising awareness of public health problems caused by harmful use of alcohol and ensuring support for effective alcohol policies. This is the soft approach since it aims to convince or persuade individuals to limit their own alcohol consumption. In practice, the government enforces laws that constraints alcohol companies and alcohol services in their relation with the clients (A known example are the health warning labels on alcohol advertisements) We can observe that in the world the most common alcohol control policy is to charge the consumer an extra fee. This makes sense as it is the easiest to enforce also for lower-income countries, who do not have the budget to have government programmes to reduce alcohol consumption We look a bit closer inside each category


Policies by regions

Observation: We observe that in the world the most common way to control alcohol consumption is through charging taxes at different scales which range from production to consumption. In fact, it is almost as important as the sum of the frequency of usage of the two other features (0.81 for Charges and 0.89 for Awareness and Restrictions). Policies linked to raising awareness of the consumer is the least used of all the means.
Explanation: Charges is one of the easiest policies to enforce since countries need not use extensive human resources to support the enacted law. Hence, it makes sense especially for lower-income countries which do not have the budget to launch governmental programmes to reduce alcohol consumption. Taxation policies are however highly subjected to critics because they are a source of income for the government. The line between security driven politics and money driven politics is often blurry, in particular in countries subjected to lobbying and corruption. Awareness probably is less popular due to the high cost of enforcement and only developed governments can afford it. The effectiveness of this method is hard to assess since psychological leverages have an important latency. It involves processes which influence the mentalities on the long term. While enacting a taxation policy or a new restriction of alcohol usage is straightforward, awareness procedures demand an actual campaign costly in time and money.
Special attention to: A possible explanation of this plot is the need of human resources. Indeed, when it comes to decision making, private and public actors are all constraints by a budget. Even in governments, where the security and health of the population should be a major concern, money is still a cross cutting issue and a high lever. Knowing that in any project a major expenditure is human resources. We can therefore reasonably carry that the more civil servants will be needed to set up the plan, the less this plan is likely to be chosen. This theory seem to adapt quite well to the data we have here.

We try to find a more accurate way to separate countries in categories and understand what influences alcohol consumption. We have several alcohol control policies and socio-economic policies that probably influence alcohol policies. We have two ways to assess the influence: train a regression model and see the weights of each feature and see the statistical correlation of each feature with alcohol consumption. ''
Even though the weights are less likely to accentuate on features that strongly correlate between themselves, they can also be less reasonable, especially if the model did not have much data to learn from, as it is in our case. For instance, the restrictions on alcohol use in parks and streets should certainly have a negative effect on alcohol consumption, but our model would put a larger weight on some other similar features (i.e. on use in leisure events) and use the given feature only for somewhat unreasonable adjustments due to correlations between said features. For that reason, we are going to use correlations with alcohol consumption directly, instead of the generated weights, even though they give some redundant information due to inter-correlations.

Latvia & Estonia

Latvia passed from 9.9 to 11.1 litres of alcohol per capita from 2010 to 2015. Estonia in this same period passed from 15.2 to 15.8. For both these neighbour countries this trend started in 2009, but ended for Estonia in 2013 for then again decrease, while in Latvia the consumption continued to rise. These changes can have many explanations. Just before 2009 we see a significant drop of GDP for both countries, of which economies alcohol consumption is contributor. It is very common for Estonians to buy alcohol in Latvia as it is cheaper, and for Finns to buy it in Estonia for the same reason. It seems that during these years there has been a game to the bottom between Estonia and Latvia on alcohol taxes, which until 2013 brought prices down significantly. In Estonia a new alcohol strategy was adopted in 2013, increasing tax rates and raising awareness. Latvia also implemented an action plan in 2012-2014, but it didn’t contain any action on alcohol taxation. We see the median age growing, but having no correlation with changes in alcohol consumption. The Education Index remains stable in Estonia, while we can observe a decrease in Latvia between the years 2008 and 2012.

Uruguay

Uruguay passed from 8 to 9 litres per capita from 2010 to 2015. Looking more into the consumption data provided by the WHO we see that this is a trend started in 2003 and accelerated in 2009. We do not find online many information available in English on Uruguay alcohol policy history, but from the GDP data it seems that the economy started growing again in 2003, at the same time as the alcohol consumption started to rise again. The Education Index dropped significantly in 2009 and got back only in 2014 to its previous level, so this could be correlated with the alcohol consumption. We see no correlation with the median age which continues to rise slowly in this period.

Cambodia

Cambodia passed from 2 to 3.5 litres per capita from 2010 to 2015, the largest increase worldwide for this period of time, although total consumption remains low compared to many countries. This country seems to have a big problem related to alcohol advertising target towards youth and for the moment is has very limited policies to regulate alcohol consumption and advertising in general. For example small and informal producers are completely excempt of any taxation framework This problem seems highly entagled with economic incentives of the advertising industry for which alcohol accounts for more than 50 percent of all advertising revenues in the country . Relating alcohol consumption in these years to GDP seems difficult to make any relevant observation as it increases between 2010 and 2012, but drops again after that. Median age rises slowly and steadily, showing no correlation with alcohol consumption and the Education Index rises from 2010.

China

We will also analyze the alcohol consumptions data in China from two perspectives: 

1. Overall level of alcohol consumption compared to other countries and the historical and cultural factors that caused this distinction.

2. Look into the trend of the alcohol consumption over time and explore the political and social reasons behind those particular turning point(as indicated by the vertical red line).

Analysis of overall level of China’s consumption 

We first observe the Time Line Chart from the  alcohol consumption data of China, and we can clearly see from it above that China's overall alcohol consumption per capita is at a rather low level, compared with the value of 10-15 units in other countries, China has always maintained the amount of around 5 units. However, It has been experienced evident increase in recent years since 1975. 

Another interesting finding is that Spirits and Beer are the most popular drink choices for Chinese people, with limited consumption of Wine. WHO estimates that 81% of Chinese alcohol consumption is in the form of spirits, 18% per cent as beer and 1% as wine.


The differences between Chinese and western wine cultures

Difference in Alcohol Type:

Baijiu (aka Chinese Spirits, or Liquor and Spirits) is the most representative alcoholic beverage in China. Chinese Spirits is made from grain. It is a strong distilled spirit, generally 52% alcohol by volume. To some extent, Chinese alcohol culture is Chinese Spirits culture. However the western people mainly drink wine.

Reason: Huangjiu ( aka Rice Wine) is one of the oldest wine in the world. About three thousand years ago, during the Shan-Chou Dynasty, the Chinese created the unique double fermentation method, and began to brew a large amount of Huangjiu. About a thousand years ago in the Song dynasty, the Chinese invented distillation, and since then, Baijiu has become the main alcohol choice for Chinese people.

Difference in Alcohol Usage:

1. For Chinese people, alcohol is more used as a tool for socialization. Chinese are highly "situation-centered," and therefore unlikely to exhibit reckless behaviour in a social setting. In the West, the purpose of drinking is often simple, drinking to appreciate wine and to enjoy fine wine.

2. Chinese traditionally drink alcohol only when eating. Drinking with food decreases the rate of alcohol absorption and may also reduce the amount consumed.

3. Both Confucian and Taoist philosophies emphasise moderation, a standard widely applied to alcohol use in China today. 

4. Traditionally when drinking Chinese play games requiring cognitive and motor skills. especially at banquets. The goal of the game is not to get drunk because getting drunk is the penalty for losing. Playing games while drinking heightens sensitivity to the state of intoxication.

Those historical and cultural factors may be the main reasons that contribute to the overall low level of alcohol consumption and difference in alcohol types between China and other three countries.


Link between the alcohol policies and the slightly increase recently

Significant turning points:

1975: At this time, alcohol consumption begin to increase after a decade of constant low level. In the past three decades, along with rapid economic growth in China, there has been a striking increase in alcohol consumption.  

1997: At this point, the alcohol consumption experienced a slightly drop after 2 decades of increase and reaching almost 5 unit per capita.

2005: Alcohol consumption recovered and begin to increase sharply, with approximately 0.6 units/year and finally surpassed 5 unit per capita in 2010

2010: Alcohol consumption reached the highest level for the whole time period and keep stable till 2015. Although the population drinking level in China used to be far lower than in many high-income and middle-income countries, per capita alcohol consumption has risen from 2·5 L in 1978 to 6·7 L in 2010. However, more than half of the Chinese population aged 15 years and older are alcohol abstainers—42% of men and 71% of women in 2010. So the alcohol consumption level of those who actually drink was 15·1 L in 2010, which is higher than the equivalent  figure in the UK, the USA, Sweden, Germany, Australia, New Zealand, and many other countries.

Policies:

1994: The Chinese Government decreased the spirits tax from a range of 40–50% to 15–25% of the value in 1994, in connection with changes required for its entry into the World Trade Organization. The drafting of this policy in 1992 was followed by an increase in alcohol consumption.

2001: A volumetric tariff of ¥0·5 per 500 g or 500 mL was added onto the existing alcohol tax as a means of raising government revenues, leading to a dramatic decrease in alcohol production and consumption after the tariff's initial announcement in 2000.

2006: The central Government lowered the tax again on spirits, introducing a unified tax rate of 20% of the value. This resulted in a steep rise in alcohol consumption again.


Conclusion

Changes in alcohol taxation have significant effects on the level of alcohol consumption in China, and also on levels of alcohol-related mortality. 10 Therefore, taxation or price policy could be considered as an effective means to reduce rates of alcohol-related health and social harms.

Compared with many other countries, there is almost no alcohol control policy in China—such provisions as licensing controls for selling alcohol, restrictions on trading hours for alcohol sales or service, and legally binding regulations on alcohol sponsorship and sales promotion are lacking. The main exceptions are limited excise taxes on alcohol products and modest restrictions on alcohol advertising. In 2006, the Chinese Government introduced a regulation to prohibit selling alcohol to minors, but the penalties for violations were not specified. Thus, a more effective minimum legal drinking age law is needed.

Please note that in the following lines, the feature “litres of averaged pure alcohol per capita per year” will be contracted under the form “L/y”.

Observations of the graph

A quick look at the graph shows that the consumption of all alcohol is kept constant at a low level (~2L/y) from 1960 to 2015. Hence the variations of the alcohol consumption are triggered by variation of the wine consumption.

1960-1999: The wine consumption has decreased almost linearly from 14 L/y in 1960 to 5 L/y 1999; leading to a decrease ~45% (=8/17.5) of the overall alcohol consumption in 39 years, i.e. a decrease of  24 cL/y per year during 39 years.

1999-2015 : The consumption is kept constant at around 5 L/y.

Hence, today wine accounts for more than half of the country’s alcohol consumption.

Link between the observations and the history of french policies

Fun fact 1: the observation of the graph are completely in accordance with the stereotype that french only drink wine.

France is a nation of drinkers. Since the Middle Ages and also favoured by the christian religion which raises wine to a sacred beverage (wine is the “blood of Jesus” while bread is its “body”), wine was highly consumed in the society (even if it was not measured). Especially in the aristocratic environment where having good wine at your table was a testimony of a high social condition.

The list of the first alcohol policies come back early in history:

1873: repression of public intoxication 

1895: teaching obligation on "the dangers of alcohol from the point of view of hygiene, morale and the social and political economy”

1914: prohibition of absinthe just before the outbreak of the war

The french government then committed into two main campaigns to reduce alcohol consumption in the XX's.  

From 1954, under the leadership of the government of Pierre Mendès France, public action against alcoholism took many forms:

Reduction of the offer by the: 

1. Closure of the drinking establishments and regulation of the points of sale (in the workplaces in particular: obligation is made to the employers to make available to their employees drinking water)

2. Development of prevention, road prevention and school prevention.

3. Organization of anti-alcohol advertising campaigns.

4. Regulation of advertising with the prohibition of associating alcohol with sports and driving.

5. Creation of the High Committee for Studies and Information on Alcoholism.


The loi Evin adopted in 1990 by the French Senate and the French National Assembly. This law empowers a set of rules to regulate advertising. The goal is to allow a presentation of the products without encouraging consumption.

- The advertisements must contain a preventive health message "Alcohol abuse is dangerous for health".

- Sponsorship operations by producers of alcoholic beverages are prohibited (at sporting, festive or cultural events for example). Only patronage is authorized

1999: alcohol is set as a psychoactive substance, i.e. considered dangerous because of its potential severe effects.

2015: decrease from 0.5 g/L to 0.2 g/L of authorised alcohol concentration in blood for all holders of a probationary license. Repression: fine and imprisonment if >0.8 g/L

2016 - loi Santé: anyone selling alcohol should require an identity card if in doubt about the consumer's age

Conclusion

The alcohol consumption decreases drastically and linearly in France since 1960. The french government enacted heavy regulations on alcohol since the XIX’s, which is very early in comparison to the other studied countries. In particular the government of Pierre Mendès France in 1954.

The french politics towards alcohol is persistent over the years: even though the alcohol is constantly decreasing since 1960, many laws are still enacted since then (loi Évin in 1990, loi Santé in 2016, etc)

Switzerland

We will structure the analysis in two ways: we start by identifying changes in the alcohol consumption in Switzerland and then try to what historical fact might have caused it (policy change, economic change or others). We then also look when alcohol policies were enacted in Switzerland and check if we can identify a change in alcohol consumption after this. 

Alcohol consumption change analysis

We start by looking at the  alcohol consumption data of Switzerland. We can identify some key moments where the alcohol consumption changed (red lines).


We identify some key moments where there is a significant change in alcohol consumption:

1965-1966: The wine consumption drops significantly, while the spirit consumption rises equally significantly, causing a stable level of litre of alcohol intake

1973-1975: In these two years there is a decrease of two litres of alcohol intake per capita, which is the fastest decrease in this graph.

1989: Beginning of a decade in which alcohol consumption continuously decreases from 14L in 1989 per capita to about 11 L per capita in 1999.

We analyse possible economic causes using a  GDP report on Switzerland. GDP did not change significantly between 1965 and 1966. In 1973 on the other hand there was a strong effect on the GDP caused by the oil crisis, so there is a correlation between slowing economy and lowering of alcohol consumption, which is the opposite what our quantitative findings have shown. In 1989, until 1999, there is a constant growth after a sudden drop of GDP in 1988.

Other indicators of the Human Development Reports cannot be analyzed in such details as they are taken usually at five years intervals.

Policy change analysis

Our first source of information are the  WHO Country profiles of Switzerland in 2011, 2014 and 2019. The difference observed are the following:

2011 ⇒ 2014: Adoption of restriction of sales in specific places and deleting restriction on time and place.

2014 ⇒ 2016: Lowering of the allowed blood alcohol concentration on young and professional drivers from 0.05 to 0.01.

In both cases the alcohol consumption decreases following a trends started before 2011 (about 2007) so it seems that these policy changes have not significantly changed the total alcohol consumption of the swiss population. This would also be the case if these changes were effective as they target a decrease of a relatively small percentage of the drinking population.

A second source of information is the  Wikipedia page of the swiss alcohol law, where we do not find any link of this law with the key moments identified in the graph. In 1997, this law was subject to a revision, which led to the liberalisation of the alcohol market and the introduction of the single tax rate: in the data above we can observe what seems a very slight increase around the year 1998, but seems insignificant.

Another significant policy action by the swiss institution is the creation of a  National Alcohol Programme, which started in 2008. There is a trend started around 2007 and 2008 that continues until today, which could be possibly be partially attributed to the programme.

Ukraine

Alcohol has always played a big part in every aspect of Slavic life. Ancient chronicles recount how in the tenth century Kyiv’s Prince Volodymyr the Great, when faced with the challenge of choosing a monotheistic religion for his people, effectively dismissed Islam due to its prohibition of alcoholic intoxication. “Wine is the joy of the Rus, and we cannot live without it” he is reputed to have uttered when told of the requirements of the Muslim faith. This passion for alcohol has remained a prominent feature of local culture and folklore ever since.

1985 The biggest government drive to cut down on Soviet drinking occurred in the early days of Mikhail Gorbachev’s period in power. In 1985 one of General Secretary Gorbachev’s first steps in power was to launch a nationwide campaign against alcohol abuse which involved the rationing of vodka alongside public awareness efforts to make drunkenness less socially acceptable.

1987 The 1985 initiative was largely abandoned after a year since it led to a sharp rise in deaths from consumption of alcohol-based industrial liquids and deadly home brews.

1994 The effects of the USSR collapse?

1996 Improving economy and the decline in the use of home brews and unrecorded alcohol make the alcohol consumption rise over the years.

2008 Ukrainian political crisis and rise in alcohol prices lead to decreasing consumption. 

2010 New laws prohibit alcohol use in public places. We see the effect of the new law in the following year, with a steep decline in alcohol consumption. Numerous other regulations were introduced around that time, prohibiting selling alcohol after 10pm etc.

Limitations

The initial goal of the project is was to make an observational study and discover which alcohol control policies are the most effective. This was not possible due to several reasons. The first hurdle was the high intercorrelation of different variables. As can be seen in the heatmap below many of the features have fairly high correlation with alcohol consumption (eg. GDP, Education index and HDI), the strong correlation among themselves mean that there is some redundancy in the information they provide us with. Partially for this reason, the previously generated weights for GDP, for example, are quite low, despite it having one of the highest correlations with the alcohol consumption.


We can also think it in terms of the following causal diagram, which illustrates how some socioeconomic-factors are linked to the government programs policies


These highly correlated variables would need to control for many factors to draw any valid conclusions. The WHO alcohol control policy dataset is limited to the year 2016, which unfortunately does not allow to control for all these parameters, as there are too few datapoints. Another feature that is important for a government when implementing a policy is to consider the cost of deploying it. In the dataset found we have no information about the this parameter, so we cannot give information about cost-effectiveness of policies. Lastly we noticed that several policies target a specific group of people, so even if effective we cannot see a variation in the total alcohol consumption. For example if a policy targets alcoholics, even if policy successful it won’t be observable in the national alcohol consumption as it is too small of a share of the population

Conclusion

In this work we discover the importance of socio-economic factors on the alcohol consumption of a country. Median age, the Education Index and slightly less GDP might have a causal effect to increase alcohol consumption. To make further investigation and draw more conclusive results access to more data of alcohol control policies is needed. In the qualitative analysis we have discovered that there the variations of alcohol consumption within a country are affected the most by changes in the tax rate of alcoholic beverages, which themselves are often affected by socio-economic factors for political reasons.
